Limitations of Optimization Models

Model Assumptions

Portfolio optimization relies on mathematical models that make simplifying assumptions, including:

  • Historical data is representative: Models assume past returns, volatilities, and correlations will persist. This is often not the case.
  • Normal distributions: Many models assume returns follow a normal distribution. In reality, markets exhibit "fat tails" with more extreme events than models predict.
  • Static parameters: Correlations and volatilities change over time, especially during market stress.
  • No transaction costs: Models typically ignore trading costs, bid-ask spreads, and market impact.
  • Perfect liquidity: Models assume you can trade any amount at quoted prices, which may not be true for all securities.

Estimation Error

Expected returns are notoriously difficult to estimate. Small errors in return estimates can lead to dramatically different "optimal" portfolios. This is known as the "error maximization" problem in portfolio optimization.

Overfitting Risk

Optimization can find portfolios that performed well historically but may not perform well in the future. This is especially true when:

  • Using short time periods for analysis
  • Optimizing with many assets and few constraints
  • Repeatedly adjusting parameters to improve backtested results
